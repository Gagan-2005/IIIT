Conclusion(Summarize key findings, observations, and learnings from the project.)This project successfully developed a high-accuracy Native Language Identification (NLI) system capable of identifying the native Indian region of English speakers using HuBERT self-supervised embeddings. Through extensive experimentation, the study demonstrated that HuBERT Layer 3 provides the most effective accent-discriminative features, significantly outperforming traditional MFCCs and deeper/lower HuBERT layers.The Random Forest classifier, combined with PCA and balanced training, achieved an exceptional 99.73% accuracy, showing that classical ML methods can outperform deep learning models when the dataset size is moderate.The project’s analyses offered several insights:MFCC vs HuBERT: MFCC features capture only low-level acoustics, whereas HuBERT embeddings capture richer phonetic and prosodic cues, resulting in far higher accuracy.Age Generalization: Models trained on adults did not generalize well to children due to physiological differences in vocal tract size, pitch, and articulation style.Word vs Sentence Level: Sentence-level speech provided more stable accent cues, whereas word-level samples were less reliable and highly variable.Layer-wise Study: Systematic evaluation of all HuBERT layers confirmed that mid-level layers (especially Layer 3) encode the most relevant accent information.Finally, the developed Accent-Aware Cuisine Recommendation System demonstrated a practical, real-world application of accent detection—showcasing how speech analytics can enhance personalization in technology.Overall, this project contributed valuable insights into accent modeling, representation learning, and practical deployment of speech-based AI systems, while also highlighting key limitations and opportunities for future research such as data augmentation, cross-age modeling, and expanding to more Indian languages.10. Future Work(Suggest potential improvements or extensions for future development.)This project achieves very high accuracy for adult speakers, but several improvements can enhance robustness, generalization, and real-world usability. Future extensions include:1. Improve Generalization to New SpeakersAlthough the model performs extremely well on trained-domain adult speech, predictions for completely unseen speakers sometimes show reduced confidence. Adding audio augmentation (pitch shift, noise, speed changes) and speaker normalization techniques can help the model focus on accent rather than individual speaker characteristics.2. Enhance Child Speech PerformanceModels trained only on adult speech struggle with child voices due to pitch and formant differences. Future work can:Collect age-balanced datasetsApply pitch/formant normalizationUse domain-adaptation techniques to reduce the adult-child mismatch3. Expand to More Indian Languages and RegionsThe current system supports 6 major regions. Expanding to regions like Punjab, West Bengal, Maharashtra, Odisha, and Northeast India would make the system more comprehensive and culturally representative.4. Develop an “Unknown Accent” CategoryReal-world users may speak accents not present in the training data. A confidence-based or probability-margin threshold can be added to classify unclear accents as “Unknown / Not Enough Information” instead of forcing one of the six labels.5. Explore Ensemble and Multi-Layer HuBERT ModelsAlthough Layer 3 performs the best, a lightweight ensemble using Layers 2–4 could further improve robustness. Multi-task learning (accent + age + gender) may also help the model learn more general audio patterns.6. Deploy as a Scalable API or Mobile ApplicationFuture versions can include:Cloud deployment (AWS/GCP/Azure)Lightweight ONNX/TFLite models for mobile devicesReal-time streaming accent detection7. Improve InterpretabilityAdding phoneme-level explanations or visualizing which parts of speech influence predictions would make the system more transparent and useful for educational applications.11.
